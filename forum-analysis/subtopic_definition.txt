T1. Test Oracle Generation (Test Oracle and Correctness Checking)

This topic covers discussions about how to determine whether the execution result of a quantum program, circuit, algorithm, logical operation, or quantum device is correct, including:

- T1.1. Verification Criteria: Using explicit expected outputs, probability distributions, statistical properties, invariants, observables, logical observables, fidelity, equivalence relations, benchmarks, or other criteria to determine whether an execution result is correct.

- T1.2. Verification without Efficient Classical Simulation: Verifying quantum-computing results, quantum-device capability, or quantum-advantage claims when the expected result cannot be efficiently calculated or simulated classically.


T2. Debug and Repair (Fault Identification and Fine-Grained Fault Localization)

This topic covers discussions about identifying, diagnosing, or localizing specific fault sources in quantum programs, QEC circuits, logical operations, or hardware, including:

- T2.1. Fault Localization: Identifying which gate, qubit, stabilizer, syndrome, detector, decoder, logical operation, or program component caused an observed failure, or seeking methods to determine the root cause of incorrect behavior.

- T2.2. Interaction-Induced Faults: Diagnosing failures caused by interactions between multiple program components, gates, qubits, errors, or execution stages, where no single component alone fully explains the failure.

- T2.3. Error Propagation: Analyzing how physical or logical errors spread, accumulate, or transform through gates, circuits, QEC procedures, or logical operations.



T3. Test Input Generation (Input Validity, Input Requirements, and State Preparation)

This topic covers constraints that quantum-program inputs must satisfy and how valid quantum or classical inputs are constructed or prepared, including:

- T3.1. State Preparation Requirements or Difficulties: Preparing a required initial quantum state, eigenstate, basis state, superposition, ancilla state, or encoded state, including practical difficulty, gate overhead, success probability, or physical preparation constraints.

- T3.2. Input Validity and Algorithm-Specific Input Requirements: Determining whether a quantum or classical input satisfies the semantic, structural, dimensional, range, or state constraints imposed by a quantum algorithm. This includes cases where arbitrary states or parameter values are not valid inputs.

- T3.3. Quantum Oracle and Problem-Instance Construction: Constructing semantically valid oracle functions, oracle circuits, classical parameters, or problem encodings for algorithms such as Grover, Simon, Shor, or quantum phase estimation.


T4. Limitations of Quantum Computers (Hardware-, Noise-, and Fault-Tolerance-Induced Constraints)

This topic covers limitations imposed by real quantum hardware, physical noise, QEC, and fault-tolerant architectures on quantum-program development and execution, including:

- T4.1. General Noise and Errors: General discussions of physical noise and errors in quantum hardware: including gate errors, crosstalk, time-dependent errors, correlated errors, imperfect syndrome measurements, measurement fidelity, and other noise effects that are not more specifically classified below.

- T4.2. Transpilation and Physical–Logical Qubit Mapping: Constraints involving basis gates, connectivity, coupling maps, routing, SWAP operations, transpilation, logical-to-physical qubit mapping, and the distinction between logical and physical circuit implementations.

- T4.3. Qubit Quality: Differences in qubit or gate quality, including calibration data, fidelity, device heterogeneity, unstable qubits, connectivity-related quality differences, and selection of reliable physical qubits.

- T4.4. Platform-Specific Constraints: Limitations that arise from a particular physical platform, such as superconducting qubits, trapped ions, photonic systems, neutral atoms, or quantum annealers, including geometry, native operations, control mechanisms, and platform-specific functionality.

- T4.5. Cost and Access: Monetary, resource, access, and limitations associated with using real or remote quantum hardware, including execution cost, usage fees, limited hardware availability, excessive queue times, job scheduling, execution quotas, service availability, and restricted access to particular devices or capabilities.

- T4.6. Error Mitigation: Methods and limitations related to reducing the effect of hardware noise without full quantum error correction, including mitigation overhead, applicability, and hardware dependence.

- T4.7. Decoherence: Limitations caused specifically by loss of coherence, including finite relaxation and dephasing times, circuit-duration constraints, and errors accumulated during idle or execution time.

- T4.8. Error Correction: Quantum error-correction mechanisms and limitations, including syndrome extraction, decoding, logical-error reliability, repeated measurement rounds, physical-to-logical qubit encoding, and the reliability of QEC under noisy hardware.

- T4.9. Fault-Tolerant: Constraints and overhead associated with fault-tolerant logical operations, including transversal gates, Eastin–Knill limitations, lattice surgery, magic-state distillation, gate teleportation, state injection, logical gadgets, and fault-tolerant resource estimation.

- T4.Others. Other hardware-, noise-, QEC-, or fault-tolerance-induced constraints that clearly belong to T4 but do not match T4.1–T4.11.


T5. Simulation-to-Hardware Gap

This topic covers differences between classical simulators and real quantum hardware, as well as efficiency, accuracy, and scalability limitations of classical quantum simulation, including:

- T5.1. Simulator Infeasibility: Cases where classical simulation becomes infeasible because of exponential state-space growth, insufficient memory, excessive qubit count, density-matrix size, tensor-network complexity, or other fundamental resource limitations.

- T5.2. Different Execution Mechanism: Differences between simulators and real hardware in how operations are represented or executed, including measurement, dynamic circuits, mid-circuit measurement, conditional execution, reset, delay, pulse-level evolution, feedback, and backend interfaces.

- T5.3. Noise Modelling: Cases where a simulator's noise model is incomplete, oversimplified, inaccurate, lacks required error mechanisms, or fails to represent the target hardware realistically.

- T5.4. Result Mismatch: Cases where the same or corresponding quantum program produces different outputs, probability distributions, expectation values, or observed behavior on a simulator and real quantum hardware.

- T5.5. Simulator Efficiency and Inefficiency: Efficiency and practical-use issues in classical quantum simulation, including excessive runtime, computational or memory overhead, slow noisy simulation, multiprocessing or distributed-simulation problems, GPU parallelization, process management, and memory distribution. This category also covers cases where Simulators Outperforming Current Hardware, e.g., simulators are faster, cheaper, more accurate, more scalable, or more practically useful than currently available quantum hardware for the studied task, provided that classical simulation has not become fundamentally infeasible.

- T5.6. Simulator-Supported but Hardware-Unsupported Operations/Information: Cases where an operation/program works on a simulator but cannot work in the same way on remote hardware. This includes simulator-only access to statevectors, unitaries, complete matrices, internal states, breakpoints, arbitrary initialization, or other operations and information unavailable on hardware.
